How Stardates Work

[century]/[year][month].[day]

To make things easier for real-life readers, stardates were set up to be just another way to write a regular date. The first number in a stardate is the century, which is based upon the year 2000. A value of "-2" would mean "1800", or the 19th century. Next there's a two-digit year followed by a two-digit month. Lastly, the day is placed after a decimal point.

Example: -3/2307.15 is the same as July 15th, 1723. That's three centuries before the year 2000, "23" is the year, "07" is the month and "15" is the day.

Since this is just a format change, there's no consideration for zero. The month still starts with "1" as the first day. If you see a trailing zero, it indicates that the day or month was omitted from the original entry. A stardate of -5/22 would be seen here as -5/2200.00 for sorting purposes.
